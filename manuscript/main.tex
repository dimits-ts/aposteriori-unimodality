\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\begin{figure}[t]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.8\columnwidth]{apunim_example.drawio.png}
	\caption{The Apunim framework attributes annotator polarization to certain characteristics.}
	\label{fig:apunim_example}
\end{figure}

Annotations are essential for a wide range of tasks, especially in fields such as content moderation, toxicity detection and hate speech detection. These tasks are especially essential for training systems that protect vulnerable and minority groups in online spaces, where they are often targeted\cite{un_hate_speech_targets, ucdavis_hate_speech}. However, annotations for such tasks are subjective and usually based on majority-group annotators. This may at best limit the usefulness of the data, or at worst lead to biased and misconfigured systems\cite{sap-etal-2019-risk}.

The core of the problem is simple; if a comment targets marginalized groups, there can be cases where most annotators overlook it, while the few marginalized annotators vehemently disagree with the majority. These fundamental disagreements are then ignored via aggregation (e.g., majority label voting) or filtering (e.g., keeping only datapoints with perfect agreement). Racist comments that are presented with coded language (usually referred to as ``dog-whistles''\cite{quaranto2022dog}) are a common example. These comments may not be picked up by most annotators, but only by ones belonging in the targeted minority. Furthermore, majority-group annotators may themselves be biased against minority groups as was the case for toxicity models disproportionally marking African American speech as ``toxic''\cite{sap-etal-2019-risk}.

\emph{Inter-annotator agreement} is often used to detect such cases\cite{petra_2022_handling}. Metrics based on agreement mostly measure annotation variance, which may not be an optimal problem formulation for detecting such cases. \emph{Polarization} is a better instrument in this regard, since it formulates annotation analysis as a cluster identification problem\cite{Pavlopoulos2023, pavlopoulos-likas-2024}. While we can detect polarization\cite{pavlopoulos-likas-2024, Pavlopoulos2023}, there is little work on how to detect whether it is actually caused by marginalized groups. \cite{pavlopoulos-likas-2024} propose a mechanism which works only for individual comments, does not produce quantifiable/comparable results, and requires the extreme case where overall polarization is zero.
We instead propose the \emph{Apunim (``Aposteriori Unimodality'') metric}, which attributes polarization to individual annotator groups. Our contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item We introduce a new metric which quantifies how much polarization can be attributed to specific annotator groups and develop a parametric test for statistical significance. This metric is generalizable across any domain and modality (e.g., video/image classification, toxicity/hate-speech detection) and enables quantitative comparisons across datasets and dimensions (e.g., gender, race, political affiliation). Unlike previous work, our metric is designed to apply to entire datasets instead of individual datapoints.
	
	\item  We discover issues not yet acknowledged in literature, such as the presence of inherent polarization and conflicting polarization directions. Our proposed metric is designed to avoid these issues.
	
	\item We apply our metric to four \ac{nlp} datasets focused on Toxicity and Hate Speech detection. We find that annotator race/ethnicity contributes to polarization across all datasets, but more so on Hate Speech tasks. We also discover that educated annotators are generally more nuanced, and that in some cases, polarization is either concentrated around similar clusters of groups (e.g., age and education) or that annotators become more polarized the more extreme their opinions become (e.g., religion).
	
	\item We release an open source Python library that implements the calculation of the metric and its statistical significance. The library can be directly installed through PyPi.\footnote{Library: \url{https://pypi.org/project/apunim/} Documentation: \url{https://dimits-ts.github.io/apunim/} }
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Annotator agreement} There are many popular metrics for measuring inter-annotator agreement such as Cohen's kappa, Krippendorff's alpha and Fleiss's kappa. All of these metrics attempt to distinguish genuine disagreement among annotators from noise (or ``random disagreement'') using statistical methods. However, a number of them can not be generally used for comparing intergroup annotation agreement without extending them (i.e., computing pairwise Cohen's kappa for all groups), or placing constraints on when they can be used (i.e., no missing labels and same number of annotations per annotator for Fleiss's kappa).  They have also been criticized for ignoring fundamental differences that can arise from different social, cultural and demographic backgrounds \cite{FEINSTEIN1990543, Byrt1993}, and for their inability to quanitfy and compare results across experiments \cite{wong-etal-2021-cross}. 

Recent work has attempted to work around these limitations. \cite{wong-etal-2021-cross} adapt Cohen's kappa for subgroup agreement, while \cite{ivey-etal-2025-nutmeg} create a Bayesian model that attempts to explicitly differentiate between subgroup disagreement and noise. While these extensions do improve the performance of these metrics, they are typically less explainable and intuitive. They are also constrained by the same basic assumptions of disagreement metrics, such as not being suitable for multi-label annotations (where annotators can pick more than one option for each item).

\begin{figure}[t]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.8\columnwidth]{disagreement_vs_polarization.png}
	\caption{Disagreement is similar to variance; thus it overlooks cases where annotators may be ``split down the middle''. Polarization on the other hand, is able to identify multiple clusters even if they are close together.}
	\label{fig:disagreement_vs_polarization}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Annotator polarization} Due to the nature of disagreement metrics, they may not perform well when comparing annotator groups with greatly mismatched sizes \cite{FEINSTEIN1990543, Byrt1993}. Figure~\ref{fig:disagreement_vs_polarization} shows an example of an annotation task where the minority group annotators may be ignored when computing disagreement, due to the overwhelming number of majority-group annotations. Polarization\cite{pavlopoulos-likas-2024} attempts to solve this problem by framing disagreement as a cluster identification problem; it specifically checks whether the annotations belong in a single cluster. This enables metrics based on polarization to work with multi-label annotations, since we are no longer comparing individual annotations against those of other annotators, but rather each annotator's histograms (see \S\ref{ssec:methodology:problem}).

Attributing polarization to subgroups is a relatively new idea. \cite{pavlopoulos-likas-2024} are the first to propose  the concept of a ``aposteriori unimodality'' for polarization; if polarization in a single comment is non-zero, but zero for all annotator subgroups, this has to mean that those subgroups are ``causing'' the exhibited polarization. Their idea is promising, but not practically usable, since overall polarization being exactly zero is an edge-case in real-life annotation tasks (\S\ref{sec:datasets}). Additionally, getting polarization estimates from realistically few annotations per datapoint is extremely noisy, which is especially problematic when the output of their framework is binary; and thus not quantifiable. \cite{sokratis} apply this binary analysis to discover whether \acp{llm} are biased in the same way as specific human subgroups. They also suggest using ordinal variables to extract ``better'' categorical labels via thresholding---the idea of treating the polarization of ordinal variables differently is promising, but we discuss the limitations of their approach in \S\ref{ssec:results:ordinal}. Their work shows that polarization is a versatile tool, but also display the constraints of using the non-quantifiable binary classification given by the traditional aposteriori unimodality framework. For instance, the authors have to resort to Cohen's kappa agreement for any kind of comparison between \acp{llm} and human annotator groups, which downgrades polarization to the role of filtering texts.


\section{How to attribute polarization}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
\label{ssec:methodology:problem}

\paragraph{Annotator groups} Since our goal is to pinpoint which specific characteristics contribute to polarization, we need to split annotators according to their \acp{pc}. Each of these characteristics is a dimension $\Theta$ composed of multiple groups $\theta_1, \theta_2, \ldots$; e.g. if we are investigating gender, then $\Theta = \{\theta_{male}, \theta_{female}, \theta_{nonbinary}\}$. In previous publications\cite{sap-etal-2019-risk, sap-etal-2022-annotators, pavlopoulos-likas-2024, dices}, these characteristics were exclusively sociodemographic in nature, although recent research confirms that personal opinions and experiences shape annotator decisions as well\cite{sokratis, kumar-et-al-2021}.

\paragraph{Annotations} Let $d = \{c_1, c_2, \ldots\}$ be a dataset composed of multiple annotated datapoints.\footnote{We use the general term ``datapoint'' instead of domain-specific terms, such as ``comments'', even though the examples presented in the paper are comments in discussions.} These datapoints could be of any modality, such as comments in a discussion, images, videos or survey questions. We assume that how polarizing a datapoint is depends on two variables: \emph{inherent (``apriori'') polarization}, and polarization caused by \emph{different annotator \acp{pc} (``aposteriori'' polarization)}. Assuming that each datapoint $c$ is assigned multiple annotators, we can define the annotation multi-set $A(c)=  \{(a, \theta) \}$, where $a$ is a single annotation. As an example, the annotations for a comment in a sentiment analysis task grouped by gender, would be formulated as:

\begin{equation}
		A(\textit{``Could be better, could be worse''}) = \{(\textit{+}, F), (\textit{---},  M), (\textit{---}, F), \ldots\}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Polarization}
Each set of annotations exhibits a certain degree of \emph{polarization}. In the absence of polarization, annotation distributions are typically unimodal: annotators may disagree on finer details---shown as a bell-shaped distribution around a central mode---rather than on fundamentally different interpretations, which would instead give rise to multimodal distributions. To our knowledge, the only polarization metric explicitly grounded in this assumption is \acf{ndfu} \cite{pavlopoulos-likas-2024}. The metric takes values in the $[0,1]$ range, with $nDFU \approx 0$ indicating no polarization (unimodal distributions) and $nDFU \approx 1$ corresponding to complete polarization (highly multimodal distributions). Our objective is therefore to quantify the extent to which the $nDFU$ of a polarizing datapoint can be partially explained by a given \ac{pc} group $\theta$.


\subsection{Intuition}
\label{ssec:methodology:intuition}

\begin{figure}[ht]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.7\columnwidth]{ndfu_combined.png}
	\caption{Example of a polarizing comment, where male and female annotators agree between themselves, but disagree with the opposite gender.}
	\label{fig:ndfu_combined}
\end{figure}


\paragraph{Polarization attribution} Intuitively, $\theta$ partially explains the polarization of a datapoint $c$ when the annotations grouped by $\theta$ are more polarized compared to the full set of annotations. Figure~\ref{fig:ndfu_combined} exhibits a hypothetical example where a misogynistic comment is annotated for toxicity by male and female annotators. The annotations are generally polarized ($nDFU_{all}=0.625$). The annotations by female annotators exhibit low polarization between themselves ($nDFU_{women}=0.1$), since most agree the data-point is toxic. The set of male annotations, also shows low polarization ($nDFU_{men}=0.3725$), but for the opposite reason---most men agree that it is \emph{not} toxic. This suggests that the overall polarization is driven by disagreements between male and female annotators.

Previous work \cite{pavlopoulos-likas-2024} stopped on a datapoint-level analysis. In practice however, this kind of analysis is inherently noisy due to a lack of information (i.e., annotations) relating to each individual datapoint. In order to get meaningful information, we need to consider information \emph{between} datapoints, as the number of datapoints is usually several orders of magnitude larger than the number of annotations per datapoint (\S\ref{sec:datasets}). In other words, instead of investigating whether a specific group becomes polarized by an individual datapoint (e.g. comment), we investigate whether that group \emph{generally becomes polarized on the specific dataset and task}.

\begin{figure*}[ht]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.32\linewidth]{ndfu_comment1.png}
	\includegraphics[width=0.32\linewidth]{ndfu_comment2.png}
	\includegraphics[width=0.32\linewidth]{ndfu_discussion.png}
	\caption{Hypothetical example of a polarizing discussion with two comments, for which the annotators disagree on which is the toxic one. If we aggregate the two comments, the polarization scores for both groups significantly rises (we need to compare two bimodal distributions), obscuring whether the exhibited polarization can be partially attributed to ethnic background.}
	\label{fig:ndfu_discussion}
\end{figure*}

\paragraph{Dataset polarization} Given this observation, we would be tempted to quantify dataset-level polarization attribution by simply aggregating all annotations in the dataset and comparing the \ac{ndfu} of each $\theta$ with the full set of annotations $A(c)$. In fact this is very tempting since aggregating annotations solves the very real and important problem of annotation scarcity; each datapoint may be annotated by only a handful of annotators (5 is a common number in many publications), leading to a best-case even split between groups (for 5 annotators, a best case scenario would be only two groups with a 3-2 split). Since \ac{ndfu} is at its core a histogram estimation, 3 annotations may not be enough to acquire a reliable histogram, and thus estimate of polarization. This is discussed in more depth in \S\ref{sec:ann_size}.

However, arbitrarily combining annotations would not work well in practice. Figure~\ref{fig:ndfu_discussion} illustrates a hypothetical discussion with two comments, both of which are toxic, but where the two groups of annotators disagree on \emph{which} comment is the toxic one. If we aggregate the annotations to a single discussion, the opposing polarization effects might balance each other out, leading to a false negative. This example demonstrates that dataset-level polarization has a \emph{direction}, which is not captured by the (symmetric) \ac{ndfu} metric.
 
 
 \subsection{Aposteriori polarization}
 \label{ssec:methodology:polstat}
 
 \begin{figure}[ht]
 	\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{overview.drawio.png}
 	\caption{An overview of how annotation information is sequentially aggregated for the \emph{apunim} metric. For each datapoint we calculate the apriori polarization and the sample aposteriori polarization given each \ac{pc} group. We then aggregate both apriori and aposteriori polarization and compare them on the dataset level to obtain the polarization attribution (``apunim'') values for each group.}
 	\label{fig:overview}
 \end{figure}

How polarizing a datapoint $c\in d$ is for annotators of group $\theta$ can be computed as $nDFU(P(A(c), \theta))$, where $P(A(c), \theta_i) = \{a(c; \theta) \in A(c) | \theta=\theta_i\}$ is the set of annotations for $c$ from annotators belonging to the \ac{pc} group $\theta_i$.

In \S\ref{sec:intro} we mentioned that annotator polarization can be caused either by the annotators \ac{pc}, or other factors. The latter can be considered ``apriori'' polarization, which is driven by the inherent ``controversy'' of the data point. Previous work \cite{pavlopoulos-likas-2024} considered the case where this apriori polarization was zero; this is not the case in many datasets, as we demonstrate in \S\ref{sec:datasets}. 

Unfortunately, we can not directly follow the intuition presented in \S\ref{ssec:methodology:intuition} and compare the differences in \ac{ndfu} between $A(c)$ and $P(A(c), \theta)$, as we would be comparing statistics between a subset and its superset (see Appendix~\ref{app:theoretical}). Comparing the polarization of groups between themselves is not an option; if we compared the two annotator groups in Figure~\ref{fig:ndfu_combined} we would conclude that there is no polarization present, since they have extremely similar \acp{ndfu}. In a scenario in which each datapoint was annotated with a very large amount of annotations, we could use bootstrapping to compare the apriori and aposteriori polarization for each comment. This however is an unrealistic assumption, since the costs of human annotation force researchers to use few annotators for each task \cite{rossi_2024}. 

To get around this limitation, we randomly partition the annotations in $\lvert \Theta \rvert$ groups, with matching group sizes,\footnote{E.g., if we have $100$ annotations, $80$ of which are made by male annotators and $20$ by female annotators, we will create random partitions of sizes $80$ and $20$.} which effectively ``breaks up'' the effect of any single isolated group on the polarization of the sample. The randomly partitioned annotations are then sampled $t$ times. Formally, the average apriori polarization for a single datapoint will be given by:

\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:pol_expected}
	\frac{1}{t} \sum_{i=1}^t  \textit{nDFU}(\tilde{P}_i(A(c)))
\end{equation}
\noindent where $\tilde{P}_i$ is the random partition operator with matching group sizes.

While the low number of annotators is an important limitation when acquiring the observed polarization \cite{rossi_2024}, this is decisively not the case with the number of datapoints themselves which can range up to hundreds of thousands. Thus, we have the luxury of filtering them before computing a dataset-wide metric. Since our metric attributes polarization, unpolarizing datapoints are useless. The same applies for datapoints which are composed of only a single annotator group. We define the subset  $S_d$ of dataset $d$ such that:

\begin{equation}
	S_d = \{c \in d \mid nDFU(c) > \alpha \textit{ and $c$ annotated by at least two groups}\}
\end{equation}
\noindent where $\alpha$ is a small positive number.

We can estimate how much polarization is generally present in the dataset, irrespective of any \ac{pc} by simply averaging the \textit{apriori} polarization of each datapoint: 
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:apriori}
	pol_{apriori}(d) = \frac{1}{\lvert d \rvert} \sum_{c \in S_d} \frac{1}{t} \sum_{i=1}^t  \textit{nDFU}(\tilde{P}_i(A(c)))
\end{equation}

Intuitively, a high apriori polarization value means that much of the polarization in the dataset is driven by factors other than \acp{pc}. In this case, we would need to observe very strong polarization within a specific \ac{pc} group in order to conclude that the overall dataset polarization can be attributed to that group. This value will be compared to the average polarization present in the dataset by annotators of \ac{pc} group $\theta$:

\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:observed}
	pol_{obs.}(d, \theta) = \frac{1}{\lvert d \rvert} \sum_{c \in S_d} pol(c, \theta)
\end{equation}

This formulation implicitly assumes that the overall polarization of a dataset can be captured by averaging polarization scores across datapoints. We consider this assumption reasonable: the mean is a simple and intuitive operator, and it exhibits appropriate sensitivity to outliers. Given that polarization values lie in the 
$[0,1]$ range, a single extreme datapoint is unlikely to substantially affect the estimate of overall polarization. In contrast, the presence of many such extreme values will meaningfully increase this estimate, which is the pattern we want to capture.

The next question would be how to compare the two values in Equations~\ref{eq:apriori},~\ref{eq:observed}. Given the inherent ``noise'' associated with most annotation tasks, a direct subtraction would not be useful. Instead we define the chance-corrected \emph{``Apunim'' (Aposteriori Unimodality)} metric as:
\begin{equation}
	apunim(d, \theta) = \frac{pol_{obs.}(d, \theta) - pol_{apriori}(d)}{1 - pol_{apriori}(d)}
\end{equation}

The scope of the statistic and its relationship with the previously presented statistics are demonstrated in Figure~\ref{fig:overview}. Since $nDFU \rightarrow [0,1] \Rightarrow apunim \rightarrow [-1,1]$. The interpretation of this metric boils down to three scenarios:

\begin{itemize}
	\item If $apunim(d, \theta) \approx 0$, the exhibited polarization among annotators of group $\theta$ does not meaningfully differ from all other groups. In other words, it is indistinguishable from noise.
	
	\item If $apunim(d, \theta) > 0$, then the polarization observed in the annotation task rises when this group is added to the annotation pool (equivalently, intragroup polarization is less than intergroup polarization). Example: If we observe $apunim(d, \textit{Afr.American}) = 0.3, apunim(d, \textit{White})=0.001$ in a hate-speech detection task, it is likely that African American annotators fundamentally disagree with white annotators on what constitutes racism in the dataset $d$. 
	
	\item Conversely, if $apunim(d, \theta) < 0$, then the overall polarization would actually \emph{rise} had we removed this group from the annotation task. Mathematically, this occurs when \emph{apriori polarization is higher than the observed polarization} (intragroup polarization is higher than integroup polarization). In other words, annotators of that group may be very polarized between themselves. Example: If we observe $apunim(d, \textit{Latino}) = -0.3, apunim(d, \textit{White})=0.001$ in a hate-speech detection task, it is likely that Latino annotators fundamentally disagree with \emph{each other} (compared to African American or white annotators) on which texts in the dataset $d$ constitutes racism. This may indicate that \emph{other dimensions (acting as confounding variables) are exerting heavy influence on the sample}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{p-value estimation} 

The condition $apunim(d, \theta) \approx 0$ is not well defined in the presence of inherent noise, which is why we need a statistical test that checks whether the deviation from 0 is significant. In other words we need to implement the following test:

\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:null_h}
	H_0: \forall \theta \in \Theta,  \textit{apunim}(d, \theta) = 0, H_i: \textit{apunim}(d, \theta_i) \neq 0
\end{equation}

For each group, we construct a null distribution of the apunim statistic by treating each randomized partition (each element in Equation~\ref{eq:apriori}, but without averaging the result) in turn as if it were the observed grouping---essentially, we compute a pseudo-apunim value by comparing its mean DFU to the mean DFU of the remaining random partitions. Repeating this process across all randomizations gives us an empirical null distribution of apunim values that reflects polarization expected by chance under the same group-size constraints. The observed apunim statistic is then compared against this null distribution using a two-sided one-sample t-test. The resulting p-value quantifies how unlikely the observed group polarization is, under the null hypothesis that annotator groups do not contribute to polarization beyond chance. The p-value estimation algorithm can be found in Algorithm~\ref{alg:pvalue}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
	\caption{P-value estimation for aposteriori polarization}
	\label{alg:pvalue}
	\begin{algorithmic}[1]
		
		\Require Dataset $d$, annotator groups $\Theta$
		\Require Filtered datapoints $S_d$
		\Require Number of random partitions $t$
		\Ensure $p$-value for testing whether $apunim(d,\theta)\neq 0$
		
		\Statex \textbf{Observed polarization} attribution
		\State
		$pol_{obs.}(d,\theta)
		=
		\frac{1}{|S_d|}
		\sum_{c\in S_d}
		nDFU(P(A(c),\theta))$
		
		\State
		$pol_{apriori}(d)
		=
		\frac{1}{|S_d|}
		\sum_{c\in S_d}
		\frac{1}{t}
		\sum_{i=1}^t
		nDFU(\tilde{P}_i(A(c)))$
		
		\State
		$obs\_apunim
		=
		\frac{pol_{obs.}(d,\theta)-pol_{apriori}(d)}
		{1-pol_{apriori}(d)}$
		
		\Statex \textbf{Null distribution}
		\State
		$Apunim_{\text{null}}
		=
		\left\{
		apunim^{(i)}
		\;\middle|\;
		apunim^{(i)}
		=
		\frac{\bar{D}^{(i)}-\bar{D}^{(-i)}}
		{1-\bar{D}^{(-i)}},
		\;
		i=1,\dots,t
		\right\}$
		
		\Statex
		where $\bar{D}^{(i)}$ denotes the dataset-level mean polarization
		obtained from the $i$-th randomized partition.
		
		\Statex \textbf{Statistical test}
		\State $p=\textit{Student-T} (Apunim_{\text{null}} \neq obs\_apunim)$
		
		\State \Return $p$
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}


Our test assumes that there exist (1) enough data-points to reliably run the means-test, and (2) enough annotations in each datapoint and \ac{pc} group to estimate the \acp{ndfu} in Equations~\ref{eq:observed},\ref{eq:apriori}.  The former can be safely assumed for most datasets, because they feature enough data-points to reliably assume normal residuals ($n \gg 30$---see \S\ref{sec:datasets}), which is why we use the Student-T test \cite{student1908probable}.
We discuss the latter assumption in Appendix \ref{sec:ann_size}.%TODO: Check whether the residuals are normal for small samples

Since we are simultaneously considering $\lvert \Theta \rvert$ hypotheses, we apply a multiple comparison correction to the resulting p-values. We choose the Holms method \cite{holms}. The test is parameterized by the \ac{FWER}, which is used to tune the strength of the correction; we can increase this value to make our test more conservative towards multiple hypotheses \cite{ChenFengYi2017}). In general, it is safe to set \ac{FWER} equal to the significance level of our test (e.g., $\textit{FWER} = 0.95$ if we are looking for $p < 0.05$).

We use four datasets from current \ac{nlp} research that include \ac{pc} information at the level of individual annotators.\footnote{While we would like to test our framework on other research areas and domains, \ac{nlp} remains the only large field to our knowledge that systematically investigates annotator biases and includes the necessary \ac{pc} information.} Specifically, we use the Kumar''~\cite{kumar-et-al-2021} and Sap''~\cite{sap-etal-2022-annotators} datasets, which are concerned with Toxicity and Hate Speech detection respectively, as well as the DICES-350 and DICES-990 datasets~\cite{dices}, which focus on LLM response safety. For the latter, we consider only labels relating to racism (\texttt{Q3\_bias\_overall}), thereby framing the task as Hate Speech detection.

While the Toxicity and Hate Speech detection tasks are not equivalent, they share substantial conceptual and operational overlap, as both require annotators to assess the presence and severity of harmful or abusive language. Consequently, the sources of annotator disagreement and bias that arise in these tasks are expected to be structurally similar, which makes them suitable for joint analysis within our framework.

The datasets differ substantially in scale and annotation design. The Kumar dataset stands out for its large size, featuring more than 100{,}000 annotated comments, as well as 10 distinct \ac{pc} and personal-experience dimensions. In contrast, the DICES datasets contain fewer annotated comments but involve more than an order of magnitude more annotators per comment than the other datasets, making them an invaluable resource for studying polarization and disagreement at scale.

A brief overview of dataset characteristics is provided in Table~\ref{tab:datasets}. Across all datasets, we observe a non-trivial degree of polarization in most annotations, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:dataset-polarization}. Finally, \emph{Age} is represented as a continuous (ratio) variable in some datasets and as a discretized interval variable in others; we discuss the implications of this difference, as well as our own discretization strategy, in Appendix~\ref{app:continuous}.

\paragraph{Polarization is consistently attributed to annotator Ethnicity across all datasets, and especially so on hate-speech detection tasks}
Across all four datasets, we observe a consistent pattern in which annotation polarization can be attributed to differences in annotator ethnicity---in many datasets this is the strongest contributor to polarization. In each case, statistically significant effects emerge for at least one of the major ethnic groups represented in the data---most notably \emph{White} and \emph{African American} annotators. Additionally, polarization contribution is substantially more pronounced in the Hate Speech datasets than in the Toxicity dataset. This suggests that \emph{ethnic background constitutes a systematic source of annotator disagreement} rather than isolated noise, and is \emph{more prevalent on tasks explicitly investigating racism}.

\paragraph{Some dimensions exert an overall influence to the dataset} 
Some \ac{pc} dimensions do not necessarily sum to zero, which suggests that \emph{these dimensions may exert a systematic influence} on the total polarization of the dataset. In other words, some annotator characteristics can consistently increase or decrease observed polarization (\S\ref{ssec:methodology:polstat}).
In the DICES-350 dataset (Table~\ref{tab:dices-350}), Asian annotators show a strong positive contribution to polarization ($apunim=0.0659$), whereas African American annotators contribute negatively ($apunim=-0.0428$), effectively reducing overall polarization. Similarly, in the Kumar dataset (Table~\ref{tab:kumar}), female annotators decrease polarization ($apunim=-0.0278$) while male annotators increase it ($apunim=0.0275$), and respondents who report having been previously targeted also increase polarization ($apunim=0.0349$), in contrast to those who have not ($apunim=-0.0633$).

\paragraph{Educated annotators tend to increase polarization} Education tends to have a moderate but consistent directional effect across all three datasets where it is present. The direction of this effect is consistent; the more educated an annotator is, the more polarized their ratings become. This may indicate that educated annotators are more likely to exhibit subjective and nuanced views.

%\paragraph{The two predominant genders display symmetrical, opposing effects} Across all datasets, the two predominant genders exhibit consistent, opposing and symmetrical effects in polarization attribution, with one group typically amplifying and the other mitigating overall polarization. In DICES-350, female annotators slightly increase polarization ($apunim=0.0215$) while male annotators reduce it ($-0.0193$), whereas in DICES-990 the direction flips, with men increasing ($0.0278$) and women decreasing ($-0.0235$) polarization. Similar patterns are observed in the Sap dataset, where male annotators elevate polarization ($0.0942$) and female annotators strongly absorb it ($-0.1810$), and in the Kumar dataset, with male annotators increasing ($0.0275$) and female annotators reducing ($-0.0278$) polarization. Nonbinary or unknown annotators consistently exhibit near-zero effects, which is likely caused by the relative low sample populations. These results suggest that while gender consistently shapes annotation polarization, the specific direction of its influence is dataset-dependent. %Admittedly, it may also indicate a possible instability of our theoretical framework on the sign of the apunim values.


\subsection{Investigating ordinal attributes}
\label{ssec:results:ordinal}

\begin{figure}[ht]
	\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{apunim_ordinal.png}
	\caption{The behavior of ordinal variables in all datasets with more than one statistically significant value. The x-axis represents the order of each factor (marked as \texttt{1), 2), ...} in Tables~\ref{tab:dices-350},\ref{tab:dices-990},\ref{tab:kumar},\ref{tab:sap}). The x-axis is normalized to the smallest common denominator between the number of ordinal values.}
	\label{fig:ordinal}
\end{figure}

Analysis on annotated data frequently ignores the different kinds of measurements in studies using sociodemographic and LIKERT-style attributes. Researchers often treat all dimensions as \emph{nominal/categorical} even though some are inherently ordinal (e.g., LIKERT-scale, education), and some continuous/ratio variables (e.g., age). Others attempt to find an ``optimal point'' which can be used to binarize continuous variables\cite{sokratis}, but this approach is misguided since by using any artificial cut-off point, the resulting analysis is immediately invalidated by any change in the underlying data. Binarization also discards valuable information not present in binary or nominal categories By treating these variables as categorical, not only do we lose valuable information, but we may magnify statistical errors\cite{Streiner2002}. We posit that \emph{retaining the order of ordinal attributes reveals interesting trends} that would be otherwise missed with categorical inference.

Figure~\ref{fig:ordinal} shows the trend lines for each ordinal \ac{pc} attribute that has at least two statistically significant values. We aggregate these trends in two patterns; \emph{``Clustering''} and \emph{``Escalating''}.

\paragraph{Clustering} Some ordinal attributes tend to cluster around a few dominant values; for example, \emph{Age} and \emph{Education} in the Kumar dataset. This pattern is expected because the Apunim algorithm is designed to identify clusters of annotations, causing annotations for nearby or similar values to be absorbed into the main effects driven by a few dominant modes.

\paragraph{Escalating} \emph{Age} in DICES-990 and \emph{Religion Importance} in the Kumar dataset follow a strict, monotonic trend. This implies that, in some cases, the \emph{amount of polarization increases the further apart an opinion is from neutrality}. For example, non-religious annotators are more divided among themselves, while very religious annotators agree among themselves, but fundamentally disagree with other annotators. 


\section{How many annotators?}
\label{sec:ann_size}

\begin{figure}[ht]
	\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{ndfu_std_error_sample_size.png}
	\caption{Standard error of the \textit{pol-statistic} when sampled with replacement for various number of annotators.}
	\label{fig:std_error}
\end{figure}

While our test works best with many annotations per datapoint, the cost required for multiple human annotators is often prohibitive \cite{rossi_2024}. It is thus imperative that we find the minimum number of annotators needed for Apunim to generate stable and reliable estimates.

We leverage the large numbers of annotations for the DICES datasets to estimate the trend of the apunim value errors. For each comment in both datasets, we randomly sample a subset of annotators with replacement and compute the apunim value. We repeat the procedure 30 times, and average all observations. This was a necessary step to reduce the variance inherent in random sampling. We also truncate the results for the DICES-350 dataset, since we did not have a large enough sample of comments annotated by 71-74 annotators.

Figure~\ref{fig:std_error} demonstrates the effect of the number of annotators on the reliability of the polarization estimation. We can see that the metric becomes consistent with about 20 annotators per comment, after which we start to get diminishing results. 


\section{Conclusion}

We introduced the ``Aposteriori Unimodality Test'', a statistical test that detects whether certain sociodemographic groups partly cause polarization in annotations. Our test is resistant to low samples of annotations, bypasses common statistical issues such as sample dependence, and avoids newly-discovered issues such as the presence of inherent polarization and polarization direction. We apply our test two human-generated and two LLM-generated datasets and find interesting patterns on the former, and verify current literature on LLM \ac{pc} prompting on the latter.
